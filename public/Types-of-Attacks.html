The Two Types of Attacks

Learn how Artificial Intelligence systems can uniquely be compromised, and how your organization can stop these attacks in their tracks. 

Previous Next

Poisoning Attacks

One of the fundamental issues with artificial intelligence and machine learning systems as a whole is the problem of "Garbage In, Garbage Out." Poisoning of training data, whether intentional or unintentional, leads to models which behave in a way not intended by the creator or user of the model. Deep learning models which are trained on flawed datasets, such as Amazon's failed hiring AI model, reflect the same flaws they were trained on. Most cases of poisoning are completely accidental, however there is a major cybersecurity risk that leveraging AI exposes. If the organization creating the model has a breach in their data security policies, they are unable to verify the integrity of the data they are using to train models. This lack of integrity means there is a chance for the attacker to inject or mis-label training data such that some perverse outcome occurs. For example, a wanted cybercriminal could hack the national facial recognition database and tag his own face under a different name to avoid detection.  

Engineers and organizations have two steps to mitigate this security risk. To protect against intentional data tampering, data protection is of utmost importance. Regular backups of data lakes and buckets should be kept, and curated datasets should have the same source control policies as source code. To protect against unintended data poisoning, ensure that engineering departments conduct appropriate testing on edge cases, and that the heuristics of predictions match the heuristics that can be expected from the data. For example, making sure the proportion of women who are hired is close in both the actual data and testing results. If there are discrepancies, this signals a poisoning risk that should be investigated.

Input Attacks

Input attacks trigger an AI system to malfunction by altering the input that is fed into the system. For example, putting black bars over small parts of a stop sign. By adding this "attack pattern," the AI is confused into mis-categorizing the input as something else. This approach is fundamentally similar to SQL injection, a well-documented problem in web application cybersecurity. Like SQL injection, a malicious pattern is able to cause adverse outcomes, such as leaking data or causing system malfunctions. In some rare cases, such as ANPR (Automated Number Plate Recognition) systems, these two attack vectors can be combined together to attack the system in more places than one. For example , a California engineer attempted to avoid fines by purchasing a "NULL" vanity plate from the DMV. He thought that the ANPR systems for parking and speed enforcment would have sanitized inputs and drop null values from their ticketing system, but was surprised with $12,000 in fines. Whenever a plate was unable to be read, he would be mailed the ticket. 
Just like with traditional applications, shielding AI applications from input attacks with validation is of utmost importance. Artificial Intelligence engineers are able to counteract input attacks by either raising confidence intervals, or having a safe default for uncertain values. For example, a self-driving car would beep and require user intervention if it was unable to read a sign with 85% accuracy.
