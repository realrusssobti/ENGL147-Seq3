Compromised AI: The Two Types of Attacks

Current consumer and industrial AI systems alike are vulnerable to unique attacks that make them less useful or stop working. Read to learn how your organization can stop these attacks in their tracks. 

Previous Next

Poisoning Attacks

“Garbage In, Garbage Out” is a common rule of thumb in the AI community. Poisoned training data causes models to behave in unexpected ways, regardless of the intent or knowledge of the poisoning. Deep learning models (models which do not have clearly defined decision patterns), such as Amazon’s failed hiring AI, reflect the flaws of the data they were trained on. Data poisoning is often a result of negligence instead of malice, but malicious actors can wreak havoc on an AI’s effectivity by poisoning data streams. 

The risk of bad actors compromising data streams is a major cyberattack risk for any organization using AI for decision-making. If the organization leaves their data sources vulnerable (through poor cybersecurity practice or otherwise), the integrity of any model trained off that data has the exact same loss of integrity as its source. 

Attackers could mis-label data, or alter the values enough to cause catastrophic damage. One hypothetical example is a cybercriminal evading face recognition by tagging their own face as some other person. 

Engineers and business leaders have two actions they can take to reduce their risks from malicious poisoning: back up data, and maintain strict source control policies. Regularly backing up old datasets allows the organization to recover quickly if they get compromised. Source control policies should mimic those of code: make sure a repository of old curated datasets is made available, tag all changes with the user that made them and the changes they made, and ensure that only the right users are able to change the main data set. A tool such as Git may be valuable here for organizations. 

Organizations must be vigilant in protecting against accidental poisoning as well. Engineers must test the edge cases, which are inputs that have a different distribution than normal. For example, making sure that a yield prediction AI produces estimates that match experimental data for all part types, instead of just checking the performance of the model as a whole. If organizations are using AI to automate decisions relating to people, they must ensure their AI is not reflecting any implicit bias against minorities. 

Input Attacks

Even when models are trained on enough data with good integrity practices, AI is known to jump to conclusions. Input attacks feed on these tendencies, altering input in a way that causes the model to mis-categorize the input as something completely different. One well-documented example is engineered grafitti on stop signs. By adding black tape to a stop sign, the AI model jumps to a conclusion based on the black bars, and reads the sign incorrectly. This attack is very similar to a SQL injection attack, which is very common on web apps. SQL injections are where a hacker sends an engineered input as their form submission, which causes unexpected actions to execute on the website’s database. The hacker’s engineered text could leak customer data, or cause widespread data loss. Like a SQL injection, AI models can be compromised by engineered inputs. This causes real-world harm, like the Tesla that crashed into the white truck because its AI wasn’t able to “see” the truck on its side. Engineered stop signs are a great example of this, as they would be able to cause fatal car accidents in the real world, and are as simple as a few pieces of tape.

Input attacks can be combined with other attacks to make them even more dangerous. A California driver tried to attack the red light/speed camera systems in the state by registering his car with the license plate “NULL.” The plan backfired, and he was assigned $12,000 in wrongly assigned fines. His license plate reading “NULL” caused all the times where the computer couldn’t make out the license plate to send him the fine. 

Protecting against input attacks is difficult, but testing models against common alterations is easy. Engineering teams should create a set of mutated and corrupted data to test their AI in, adding random noise and patterns that may cause problems. This would give the organization a better idea of when AI is going to run into problems. Organizations should also make their AI safer by having a safe default condition. Depending on the algorithm design, this would either involve having the user decide, or discarding the prediction. For example, self-driving cars could beep when their self-driving systems cannot read their surroundings and require human input.
